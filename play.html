Surfing

Surfing has been a life-long passion of mine. I can't remember exactly when I learned, but I was young enough that my mom wouldn't let me use a surfboard -- so I surfed on my family's boogie boards. Eventually I received hand-me-down surfboards from my sister's and brother's friends. Waves are special. I remember waves. My first wave was bumpy and wild whitewater ride. My board was squirrly. It swerved right and left while I slowly got to one knee and finally up to both feet. It was late in the day. The sky was dark and the water was darker. My mom and dad and my aunt Joan were watching fom the beach. It wasn't a good wave, but it was my first. And everyone remembers their first. My brother taught me how to go left and right on waves. It was again late in the day and late in the summer. In fact, it was the last day of summer. The next day I'd be sitting in class. I couldn't read the waves to know if I should go right or left. As I paddled for each wave, David would yell "RIGHT!" if I need to turn right. I wasn't very good, so when I turned right, I kept turning, and inadvertenly pulled out of every wave. The next summer I had a new board. It was a "RAW Seasoned" surfboard. When I grew out of it, I'm told, Eva, Audrey, and their friends knew it as Peyton's board. But that summer it was still mine. That summer I caught my first wave where I turned right, but managed to stop turning. It was a small wave -- I was small, and the wave was only waist-high on me. The good thing about being small in Virginia Beach is that small waves are okay. I never made it to the open face on that wave, but I did make it down the line.

Skimboarding

Long stretches of small waves and flat oceans demand that surfers in Virginia Beach have other ways to get their adrenaline fix. I started skimboarding to pass the time during these flat spells.
