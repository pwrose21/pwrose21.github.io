Graduate Research

For my PhD research, I analyzed data collected by the ATLAS experiment at the Large Hadron Collider (LHC).
The LHC is a circular particle accelerator with a circumference of 27km that collides counter-circulating beams of protons at a center-of-mass energy of 13 TeV.
ATLAS is one of four major experiements located at interaction points around the LHC.
Specifically, I was involved in searches for the decay of the Standard Model Higgs boson to bottom quarks. Higgs bosons are the tangible evidence for the theory that explains the origin of mass in the universe, and were discovered at the LHC in July 2012. These particles, however, are unstable, meaning that after being produced, they quickly decay to more common forms of matter (e.g. electrons), and about half of the Higgs bosons that we produce are expected to decay to a pair of particles known as bottom quarks.
My research looked for this dominant decay, which is particularly difficult to observe because the particle collisions that produce Higgs bosons also produce many orders of magnitude more particles which can mimic their decay.
We used multivariate and statistical techniques to develop a new search for this decay. Our seach targets the vector boson fusion production mechanism in association with a photon, and it benefits from the large dataset collected by the ATLAS experiment in 2016.
We trained a boosted decision tree using simulated Monte Carlo samples to discriminate between signal (i.e. Higgs boson decays) and background (everything else) signatures in our detector, and then we used a profile likelihood fit to measure the compatibility of our observed data with the background only hypothesis.
As of 2016, the observation of the Higgs boson decay to bottom quarks still remains elusive, but we were able to set new limits on how often this process occurs.
